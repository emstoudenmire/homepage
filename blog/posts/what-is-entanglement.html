What Is Entanglement, Anyway?

October 20, 2024

"Spooky action at a distance." Einstein's famous phrase has done more to confuse people about entanglement than almost anything else. In this post I want to give a clearer picture of what entanglement actually is, why it matters, and how it connects to tensor networks.

Entanglement is about correlations

At its core, entanglement is a statement about correlations between parts of a quantum system. If you have two particles in an entangled state, then measuring one particle gives you information about what you'll find when you measure the other. That much is true of classical correlations too—if I put one red ball and one blue ball in two boxes, opening one box tells me what's in the other.

What makes quantum entanglement special is that the correlations are stronger than anything possible in a classical system. This was formalized by John Bell in 1964, and has since been confirmed experimentally many times.

Why it matters for tensor networks

The amount of entanglement in a quantum state directly controls how efficiently that state can be represented as a tensor network. A state with little entanglement (following an "area law") can be well-approximated by a matrix product state with modest bond dimension. A state with a lot of entanglement requires exponentially large bond dimensions and can't be efficiently represented this way.

This is why DMRG works so well for ground states of one-dimensional systems: these states typically obey an area law, meaning their entanglement is bounded regardless of system size. It's also why simulating highly entangled states (like those produced by a quantum computer running a deep circuit) is so hard classically.

Entanglement as a resource

In quantum information theory, entanglement is treated as a resource that enables tasks like quantum teleportation and superdense coding. From the tensor network perspective, the entanglement structure of a state tells you which tensor network geometry is most appropriate for representing it. Understanding entanglement is really the key to understanding when and why tensor network methods succeed.
