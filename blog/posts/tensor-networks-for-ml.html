Why Tensor Networks for Machine Learning?

December 3, 2024

Deep neural networks have been spectacularly successful, so why would anyone look for alternatives? In this post I want to explain why tensor networks are a genuinely interesting class of models for machine learning, not just a curiosity from physics.

The connection to quantum states

A quantum wavefunction over N qubits is described by 2N complex amplitudes—an exponentially large object. Tensor networks give us a way to represent the physically relevant corner of this space using a number of parameters that grows only polynomially in N. The insight is that physical states have limited entanglement, and tensor networks are precisely the right language to exploit that structure.

The same idea applies to data. If the important patterns in a dataset have a structure that can be captured by local correlations (analogous to limited entanglement), then a tensor network model can represent the relevant function efficiently.

Advantages over neural networks

Tensor network models have some appealing properties:

  • Interpretability: the internal structure of a trained tensor network can be analyzed using tools like entanglement entropy, giving insight into what the model has learned.
  • Training algorithms: methods like DMRG provide powerful, well-understood optimization strategies that differ from gradient descent.
  • No barren plateaus: the loss landscape of tensor network models tends to be better-behaved than that of very deep networks.

Where this is headed

Tensor network ML is still a young field, and there are many open questions about how to scale these models and which problem domains they're best suited for. But the theoretical foundations are strong, and I think we'll see exciting developments in the coming years.
